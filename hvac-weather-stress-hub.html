Coastal HVAC Science

Why Marine Layer Humidity Hurts Your Compressor

Your AC doesn't just cool air — it dehumidifies it. Every pound of moisture removed from the air costs energy. Coastal San Diego's sustained 75–90% relative humidity in summer forces your system to do twice the moisture-removal work before it can cool effectively.

Coil freeze risk: Running your AC at 68°F or below when outdoor temps are 62–68°F (marine layer mornings) is the #1 cause of frozen coils in coastal neighborhoods. The fix: set your thermostat to 72°F minimum when it's foggy outside, or switch to fan-only mode in the morning.

Repair vs Replace

The Honest Decision Ladder

Run down this list in order. The answer will be clear before you finish.

1

How old is the unit?

Under 8 years: almost always repair. 8–12 years: depends on repair cost. Over 12 years: lean replace unless repair is under $400. San Diego's coastal salt air cuts rated lifespan by 3–5 years — adjust accordingly.

2

What refrigerant does it use?

R-410A: fine, continue repairing. R-22 (older systems, phased out 2020): stop repairing. R-22 refills cost $50–100/lb and supply is shrinking. Any significant leak on an R-22 system = replace.

3

What's the repair cost vs 5-year run cost?

Rule of thumb: if repair cost exceeds 30% of a new system's price, replace. A new 2.5-ton central system installed in North County runs $5,500–8,500. 30% = $1,650–2,550. If the quote is above that range, get a replacement estimate in the same call.

4

Has it broken down more than once in 18 months?

Two breakdowns in 18 months is a pattern, not bad luck. Get the full diagnostic report. If two separate components have failed, the system is telling you something. Don't keep paying for triage.

5

Did you get a second opinion on any quote over $800?

HVAC markups in San Diego range from reasonable to 3× fair value. For any repair over $800 or any full replacement, a second quote takes one phone call. SideGuy can help you read the first quote before you call the second company.

Emergency Action Plan

Your AC Just Quit — Do This Tonight

1

Check the circuit breaker first

HVAC systems have two breakers — one for the air handler (inside), one for the condenser (outside). Both should be fully ON. A tripped breaker is not the same as a dead unit. Reset once. If it trips again immediately, stop and call — there's a short somewhere.

2

Check for ice on the unit

If the air handler coil is frozen (ice visible on lines or the unit), turn the system OFF and switch to fan-only. Let it thaw 2 hours before calling anyone. Running a frozen coil burns out compressors. Common during marine layer mornings.

3

Check your air filter

A completely clogged filter can cause a system to shut down on safety. Pull it out. If it's grey and dense, replace it ($10 at any hardware store) and restart. This fixes a surprising number of "AC broke" calls.

4

Call your HVAC company — ask specifically about after-hours diagnostic fees

After-hours diagnostics in North County run $95–200 just to show up. Some companies charge this on top of the repair. Ask before they arrive: "What is the diagnostic fee and does it apply toward the repair?" Get the answer in writing via text.

5

Don't approve anything over $400 without a written itemized quote

Verbal approvals in the moment lead to invoice surprises. Ask them to text or email the breakdown before work starts. Any reputable North County company will do this without hesitation. If they push back, that's signal.
