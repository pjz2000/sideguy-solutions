HVAC Quote Seems Too High in San Diego

Before you accept or reject a quote, get a clear picture of what's normal — and what's not. Real San Diego numbers, no sales pitch.

System replacement costs (San Diego)

  • 2–3 ton central A/C: $6,000–$10,000
  • 4–5 ton central A/C: $9,000–$14,000
  • Mini-split (per zone): $3,000–$7,000
  • Heat pump system: $8,000–$16,000

Common repair costs

  • Tune-up / maintenance: $80–$200
  • Capacitor / contactor: $150–$350
  • Refrigerant recharge: $150–$400
  • Blower motor: $400–$800

Red flags in a quote

  • Pressure to decide today
  • No itemized breakdown
  • No mention of permits
  • No load calculation for replacement
Rule of thumb: If a repair quote is more than 50% of the cost of a new unit, a replacement conversation is worth having. But don't let a contractor make that decision for you.

What a Legitimate Quote Includes

A professional, trustworthy HVAC quote should always include:

  • Equipment brand, model number, and SEER rating — this is the single most important comparison point between quotes
  • Tonnage / system size — based on a load calculation (Manual J), not just "what was there before"
  • Labor hours and technician rate — itemized, not bundled
  • Permit fees — San Diego requires permits for HVAC replacement work
  • Warranty terms — both equipment warranty and contractor labor warranty
  • Duct modification or electrical upgrade costs — listed separately so you can see what's driving cost
Red flag: Any contractor who suggests skipping permits is protecting their workflow, not yours. Unpermitted HVAC work can void manufacturer warranties, create issues during home sales, and sometimes affect homeowner insurance claims.

How to Compare Quotes Fairly

Getting three quotes is good advice — but only if you're comparing the same things. Here's how to do it right:

  1. Ask each contractor for the same equipment tier — give them the model number from the first quote, or at minimum the brand, tonnage, and SEER rating you want
  2. Confirm permits are included in every quote — not as an add-on
  3. Ask each contractor to show you the Manual J calculation — this proves they sized the system correctly for your home
  4. Compare warranty terms — some contractors offer 1-year labor, some 5 years. This affects total value significantly
  5. Ask about rebates — SDG&E and California have efficiency rebates that can cut $200–$1,500 off qualifying equipment

When to Repair vs Replace

A common decision. A few honest rules:

  • Repair if: the unit is under 10 years old AND the repair is under $800 AND it involves a simple part (capacitor, contactor, blower motor)
  • Consider replacing if: the unit is over 12–15 years old, the refrigerant is R-22 (phased out), or any single repair exceeds 40–50% of replacement cost
  • Definitely compare if: a contractor recommends replacing the coil or compressor on a unit over 10 years old — at that point, the math rarely favors repair

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should an HVAC replacement cost in San Diego?

A standard central air system (2–5 ton) with installation typically costs $6,000–$14,000 depending on size, brand, efficiency rating, and duct work needed. Mini-split installation runs $3,000–$7,000 per zone. If a quote is significantly outside these ranges, ask for a full itemized breakdown.

What makes an HVAC quote legitimate vs inflated?

Legitimate quotes include itemized parts and labor, system model and SEER rating, permit costs, a load calculation (Manual J), and warranty terms. Red flags: pressure to decide same-day, refusal to itemize, heavy upsell on unnecessary add-ons, or skipping permits.

Do I need a permit for HVAC replacement in San Diego?

Yes. Most HVAC replacement work in San Diego requires a permit. A contractor who suggests skipping it is protecting their time, not yours. Unpermitted work can affect insurance claims, home resale, and equipment warranties.

How do I compare HVAC quotes fairly?

Compare the same system size (tonnage) and SEER rating across quotes. Ask each contractor to provide a Manual J load calculation. A cheaper quote for a smaller or lower-efficiency unit is not actually cheaper — compare the equipment specifications, not just the total price.

What should a San Diego HVAC tune-up or repair cost?

Seasonal tune-up: $80–$200. Refrigerant recharge: $150–$400. Capacitor or contactor: $150–$350. Blower motor: $400–$800. Evaporator coil: $800–$2,200. Any single repair over $1,500 should prompt you to compare against replacement cost.
