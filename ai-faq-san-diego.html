AI FAQ — practical answers for small teams

Answers and examples to help you decide whether a pilot is right for your team, what to expect, and how to measure success.

Who should read this

Operators, small business owners, and technical leads who need plain-language answers about costs, risk, and pilot design.

Common short answers

How long does a pilot take?
2–6 weeks depending on scope.
How much does it cost?
$2,500–$8,000 for a typical pilot.
Do we need special staff?
No—pilots can be run without a full-time ML engineer; we focus on practical automations.

How to evaluate claims

Vendors often promise dramatic improvements. Ask for: sample inputs/outputs, an explanation of failure modes, and clear acceptance criteria. A short pilot reduces vendor risk and gives evidence for scaling.

Privacy & compliance basics

Document data types, set access rules, and define retention. Use synthetic or anonymized data for early experiments, and add technical and legal checks before moving to production for regulated data.

Measuring success

Pick clear metrics (time saved, percent automated, error rate). Track them before and after the pilot, and set a decision point for scaling.

Example: If auto-responses correctly handle 85% of simple inquiries and reduce agent triage time by 60%, that’s usually a strong signal to proceed.

Common questions (expanded)

How do you handle incorrect outputs?
We include fallback rules (escalate to human reviewers), logging for error analysis, and a short retraining loop if needed.
What if my data is messy?
We clean and sample data during discovery, and the pilot usually includes a small data-prep step to ensure quality.
Can AI handle customer disputes or legal questions?
No — avoid using AI for high-risk legal or regulated decision-making; use AI to triage and flag for human review instead.
How do you measure ROI for non-monetary gains?
Track human-hours saved, response speed improvements, and any downstream revenue or retention effects tied to faster or more accurate handling.
