Solar Installation Cost in San Diego: What You'll Actually Pay in 2026

SDG&E has the highest electricity rates in the continental US. Solar math in San Diego is different from the rest of the country — and NEM 3.0 changed it again. Here's what things actually cost, what red flags to watch, and how to run your own numbers.

NEM 3.0 — what changed and what it means for you

NEM 3.0 became effective for new SDG&E interconnection applications after April 15, 2023. If you signed up before that date, you're on NEM 1.0 or 2.0 for up to 20 years — that's valuable, don't give it up by switching systems without careful analysis.

Under NEM 3.0:

  • Export credit averages ~$0.05/kWh (was $0.30–0.55/kWh under NEM 2.0)
  • You still consume solar at full retail rate avoidance (~$0.40–0.55/kWh value during the day)
  • Exporting excess is nearly worthless. Self-consumption is everything.
  • Battery storage maximizes self-consumption — charge during solar peak, discharge in the evening peak pricing window

Under NEM 3.0, solar without a battery has a 9–13 year payback in San Diego (vs 6–8 years under NEM 2.0). Solar + battery with good self-consumption strategy: 7–10 years payback. The battery is no longer optional if you're optimizing for ROI.

5 Signs Your Solar Quote Is Too High

San Diego's 2026 average is $2.80–$3.40/watt installed. Here's what to watch for:

  1. Price per watt above $3.80 — If you're seeing numbers north of $3.80/W for a standard residential system, you're being overcharged. Get a second quote.
  2. Installer won't show you the itemized quote — A professional will give you a line-by-line breakdown: panels, inverter, racking, labor, permitting, and utility interconnection fees. If they refuse, walk away.
  3. Pushing a 25-year loan with high APR — Solar loans with 8–12% APR over 25 years add 60–80% to the total cost of the system. A 5–7 year payback can stretch to 15+ years once interest is factored in.
  4. No mention of NEM 3.0 and battery storage interaction — Any installer quoting you in 2026 without discussing NEM 3.0 is either uninformed or hoping you don't ask. This is the single biggest factor in your payback math.
  5. Can't answer: "What is your panel degradation warranty?" — Tier 1 panels (Silfab, REC, Panasonic, Maxeon) carry 25-year performance warranties guaranteeing 80–87% output. If the installer can't cite the warranty spec, the panels aren't Tier 1.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does solar cost in San Diego in 2026?

The 2026 installed price in San Diego runs $2.80–$3.40 per watt. A typical 8 kW system costs $22,400–$27,200 before the 30% federal tax credit, or $15,680–$19,040 after credit. Use the calculator above to estimate your specific system size based on your SDG&E bill.

Is solar worth it in San Diego with NEM 3.0?

Yes, but the math changed significantly in April 2023. Under NEM 3.0, export credits dropped from ~$0.35/kWh to ~$0.05/kWh — a 75% reduction. Solar-only payback stretched from 6–8 years to 9–13 years. With a battery optimizing self-consumption, payback drops back to 7–10 years. SDG&E's rates ($0.40–0.55/kWh peak) are still among the highest in the US, making self-consumed solar highly valuable.

What is a good price per watt for solar in San Diego?

In 2026, a fair price per watt installed in San Diego is $2.80–$3.40 for a standard residential system with Tier 1 panels and microinverters. Above $3.80/watt is a red flag. Below $2.60/watt warrants questions about panel quality and warranty terms. Always compare 3 proposals.

Should I add battery storage with my San Diego solar?

Under NEM 3.0, a battery is no longer optional if you want to optimize ROI. Without a battery, excess solar gets exported at ~$0.05/kWh — nearly worthless. With a battery, you charge during solar peak and discharge during SDG&E's evening peak pricing window (~$0.50/kWh), making each stored kWh ~10x more valuable than the export rate. The 30% ITC also applies to the battery.

How long does solar take to pay off in San Diego?

Under NEM 3.0 in 2026: solar-only payback is 9–13 years; solar + battery is 7–10 years. Cash buyers with the 30% ITC on both solar and battery see the shortest paybacks. Loan customers should factor in dealer fees (3–8%) and interest rate — a 9% APR 25-year loan can effectively double the payback period compared to cash.
