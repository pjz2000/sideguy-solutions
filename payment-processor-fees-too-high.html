Payment Processor Fees Too High

Before switching processors or accepting a new deal, understand what you're actually paying — and what's normal. Most businesses don't know their effective rate.

What's a normal effective rate?

  • Card-present (swipe/tap): 2.0–2.8%
  • Card-not-present (online): 2.5–3.2%
  • Keyed-in transactions: 2.8–3.5%
  • Over 3.5% on card-present: too high

Common junk fees to find

  • Non-qualified surcharge
  • Regulatory compliance fee
  • Annual fee (separate from monthly)
  • Batch fee, statement fee, IRS fee

How to calculate your rate

  • Total fees ÷ total volume = effective rate
  • Example: $320 fees / $11,000 volume = 2.9%
  • Both numbers are on your statement
  • Do this every month — rates can creep
Quick check: Pull last month's merchant statement. Find "total fees" and "total volume." Divide fees by volume. If the result is over 3.0% for in-person sales, you're likely overpaying.

Understanding the Pricing Models

The way your processor charges determines how much you can actually control your costs. Three main models:

Pricing modelHow it worksBest forWatch out for
Flat-rateOne rate for all card types (e.g., 2.6% + $0.10)Very small businesses, low volumeExpensive as volume grows; you're subsidizing rewards cards
TieredQualified / mid-qualified / non-qualified tiersAlmost nobodyNon-qualified surcharges are where processors make margin; opaque
Interchange-plusActual interchange cost + fixed markupBusinesses over $10K/monthStatements are more complex, but it's the most transparent model
Subscription / flat monthlyMonthly fee + interchange onlyHigh-volume businessesOnly saves money above a certain volume threshold
The tiered pricing trap: If your statement has lines like "non-qualified surcharge" or "mid-qualified rate," you are on tiered pricing. Most business cards and rewards cards get downgraded to non-qualified automatically, triggering a hidden surcharge. Ask to switch to interchange-plus.

What to Look for on Your Merchant Statement

A month-by-month audit takes about 10 minutes once you know what you're looking at:

  1. Find your effective rate — total fees ÷ total volume. Know this number cold.
  2. List every fee line — anything that isn't "interchange," "assessment," or "monthly service fee" deserves scrutiny
  3. Look for rate creep — processors can raise rates with 30–90 days notice buried in a mailer. Compare your effective rate to 6 months ago
  4. Check your contract — note when your term ends and what the cancellation fee is. This affects your negotiating leverage
  5. Count your monthly fees — PCI compliance, statement fees, batch fees. If you have more than 3 monthly fixed fees, you can likely negotiate some out

How to Negotiate Lower Fees

Most businesses never ask — and processors know it. This means there is often real room to negotiate:

  • Start with your effective rate, not the listed rate. Tell them your effective rate and ask what they can do.
  • Mention competition. Get one competing quote and tell your current processor. You don't have to switch — just let them know you're looking.
  • Ask to remove specific junk fees — statement fees, batch fees, regulatory compliance fees are often negotiable or removable on request.
  • Request interchange-plus pricing if you're currently on tiered. Many processors will switch existing accounts without requiring a new contract.
  • Know your termination date. You have maximum leverage when your contract is month-to-month or within 60 days of term end.

When to Switch Processors

Switch if:

  • Your effective rate is above 3.0% on in-person transactions and your current processor won't move
  • You're on tiered pricing and they won't offer interchange-plus
  • You have a long-term contract with high termination fees — wait until it expires, then move
  • You're processing over $20,000/month on flat-rate pricing (Square, stripe flat) — you're likely leaving $1,000–$3,000/year on the table
  • Your processor charges for chargebacks above $25 each with no dispute support

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a normal credit card processing rate for a small business?

For most small businesses, an effective rate of 2.2–2.8% for card-present transactions is competitive. Card-not-present (online/phone) typically runs 2.5–3.2%. If your effective rate is consistently above 3.0%, investigate your pricing model and any junk fees on your statement.

What fees on a merchant statement are junk fees?

Common junk fees: non-qualified surcharge (on tiered pricing), regulatory compliance fee, statement fee, batch fee, IRS reportin fee. These can add 0.5–1.0%+ to your effective rate without providing any actual service.

What is interchange-plus pricing and why does it matter?

Interchange-plus (cost-plus) pricing shows you the actual interchange rate set by Visa/Mastercard plus a fixed markup. It's the most transparent model. Tiered and flat-rate pricing bundles these costs in ways that often hide how much the processor is marking up. Most businesses processing over $10,000/month benefit from switching to interchange-plus.

How do I calculate my effective processing rate?

Divide total fees paid by total volume processed. Example: $350 in fees on $12,000 in sales = 2.92%. Both numbers are on your monthly merchant statement. Track this every month — rates can creep up quietly.

When should I switch payment processors?

Consider switching if your effective rate is above 3.0% on card-present and the processor won't negotiate, you're on tiered pricing with no option to change, or you're processing over $20,000/month on flat-rate pricing (like Square or Stripe flat-rate).
