Autonomous Energy Systems

Combining solar, batteries, and AI-managed dispatch, homes and commercial buildings are increasingly operating as energy assets — not just energy consumers. Here's how the automation actually works today.

Layer 1: physical
  • Solar array (production)
  • Battery storage (Powerwall, Enphase IQ, sonnen)
  • Smart inverter + grid meter
Layer 2: software
  • Energy management system (EMS)
  • Forecast-based charging/dispatch logic
  • VPP control interface
Layer 3: market
  • Utility net metering credits
  • VPP dispatch payments
  • Demand response programs

How the automation actually works

A modern home energy system doesn't just react — it predicts. Tesla's Powerwall software, for example, pulls CAISO day-ahead pricing forecasts and weather data to decide when to charge from solar vs. the grid, and when to hold charge for potential dispatch events (peak-pricing windows when discharging earns more).

In a virtual power plant (VPP) program, the utility or aggregator can send a dispatch signal — "discharge now, grid needs capacity" — and the system responds automatically within seconds. The homeowner gets paid per kWh discharged, typically credited monthly to their utility bill or paid directly.

Commercial buildings add another layer: building management systems (BMS) that integrate HVAC, lighting, and on-site generation into a single automated demand-response strategy. A large commercial customer can reduce peak demand charges significantly through automated load-shifting orchestrated by the BMS without any human intervention.

EV integration: the third asset

Electric vehicles with bidirectional charging capability (V2G — vehicle-to-grid) add a third energy asset to the system. The car's battery can be discharged to power the home during peak rates, or dispatched to the grid during critical events, then recharged overnight at low-cost off-peak rates.

Ford F-150 Lightning, Nissan LEAF (V2G version), and several upcoming EV models support this. The software layer to manage this automatically — coordinating car charging with solar production, home demand, and grid pricing — exists today in prototype form and is being commercialized by several companies.

Commercial building automation

Commercial buildings over ~50,000 sq ft typically have sophisticated BMS platforms (Johnson Controls, Siemens, Honeywell) that already automate HVAC, lighting, and access systems. Integrating energy storage and solar management into these systems creates a demand-response platform that can:

  • Shift cooling load to off-peak hours when electricity is cheaper
  • Pre-cool the building before peak rate periods begin
  • Discharge storage during the most expensive demand periods
  • Automatically respond to utility demand response signals

Large commercial customers in California can achieve 15–25% reductions in peak demand charges through automated load management — representing significant savings since demand charges often comprise 30–40% of a commercial electric bill.

The path to fully autonomous energy trading

Today's systems are semi-autonomous — they respond to utility signals and optimize within preset rules. The next step (direct participation in wholesale energy markets at the residential level) requires regulatory changes that are in progress in New York, California, and Texas but not yet available to most homeowners. Fully peer-to-peer energy trading between homes, managed by smart contracts with automated settlement, is a 5–10 year horizon. The infrastructure layer being built now will enable it.

FAQ

What hardware do I need to participate in a VPP program?+
A grid-tied solar system plus a compatible battery with smart inverter. Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery, sonnen, and some LG RESU units are commonly supported. The battery must have internet connectivity and support for utility dispatch signals. Your installer can confirm compatibility with available programs in your utility territory.
Does the automation work during a power outage?+
Systems configured for backup power will automatically island from the grid and power critical loads from the battery during an outage. VPP dispatch events don't occur during outages — the system reverts to backup mode. This is managed automatically by the inverter and BMS without any user action.
How does AI decide when to charge vs. discharge?+
Modern systems use a combination of: historical usage patterns, weather forecasts (to predict solar generation), utility rate schedules (time-of-use pricing), and VPP event probability models. The EMS optimizes charge/discharge decisions to minimize your net electricity cost while maintaining preset backup reserves. Most systems allow users to override default settings through an app.
Can a small business participate in demand response programs?+
Yes, and it can be significant. SDG&E's Business Energy Advisor and PG&E's Business programs offer demand response incentives for commercial customers with controllable loads (HVAC, lighting, EV charging). Businesses with 50+ kW peak demand can often qualify for structured programs that pay for pre-agreed curtailment during grid stress events.
