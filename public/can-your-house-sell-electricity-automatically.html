Can Your House Sell Electricity Automatically?

Solar + batteries already let homes push surplus power to the grid. The next step — software that decides when and how to sell that power automatically — is closer than most people realize.

Where it stands today
  • Net metering already pays homeowners for excess solar
  • Virtual power plants (VPPs) are live in CA, TX, and several other states
  • Tesla, Sunrun, and utilities are running real programs now
What's still developing
  • Real-time pricing response (sell when prices peak)
  • AI-managed dispatch decisions
  • Automated machine-to-machine settlement
Key requirements
  • Solar array + battery (Powerwall, Enphase, etc.)
  • Smart inverter with grid-export capability
  • Utility program enrollment or VPP contract

How the money currently flows

Under standard net metering, your utility credits your bill when your solar system pushes power back to the grid. California's NEM 3.0 (active since 2023) shifted from retail-rate credits to avoided-cost rates, which are lower — but time-of-use rates mean selling during peak evening hours still pays meaningfully.

Virtual power plants go further. Programs like Tesla's VPP in California and Sunrun's GridServices actively dispatch your battery during grid stress events — and pay you per kilowatt-hour discharged. Homeowners in these programs report $200–$600/year in payments depending on battery size and local grid conditions.

What a virtual power plant actually does

A VPP aggregates hundreds or thousands of home batteries into a single dispatchable resource. When the grid needs power — say, during a heat wave when demand spikes in the afternoon — the VPP operator sends a signal to every enrolled home battery, telling it to discharge to the grid simultaneously.

From the grid's perspective, this looks like a power plant responding to demand. From the homeowner's perspective, the battery drains partially, the home stays powered from the grid during the event, and a payment lands in the account. The whole thing happens automatically with no homeowner action required.

California ISO (CAISO) and ERCOT in Texas already integrate VPP capacity into their grid planning. PG&E, SDG&E, and other utilities now offer incentive programs specifically to enroll home batteries into these systems.

Where AI fits in

The current generation of VPPs uses rule-based dispatch: batteries discharge when the utility sends a signal. The next layer adds forecasting. Tesla's software already predicts when grid events are likely based on weather, demand history, and wholesale price forecasts — and pre-charges the battery in anticipation.

Fully autonomous decisions (system decides on its own when to charge, hold, and sell based on real-time market pricing) require access to wholesale electricity markets, which are currently restricted to large generators. Several states are working on retail-level access that would let home systems participate directly — but as of early 2026, most homeowners still need to go through a VPP aggregator or utility program.

Bottom line

If you have solar + battery in California today, you can already participate in a program that pays you to discharge automatically. The "fully autonomous AI-managed energy business" version is a few regulatory cycles away. The more immediate question is whether enrolling in a VPP program makes sense for your specific setup and utility — that math varies significantly by location and battery size.

FAQ

Can I enroll in a VPP program if I have a Tesla Powerwall in San Diego?+
Yes. SDG&E has a Connected Solutions program, and Tesla's own VPP program operates in California. Eligibility depends on your Powerwall firmware version and whether your utility territory participates. Check Tesla's app under the "Energy Programs" tab for current enrollment status.
How much does a VPP program typically pay?+
Varies widely. California VPP programs have paid $0.20–$0.50 per kWh discharged during peak events, with annual payments typically ranging $200–$600 for a 13.5 kWh Powerwall. Some programs also offer upfront enrollment incentives of $100–$400.
Does participating in a VPP damage my battery?+
Most VPP programs are designed to limit discharge depth to preserve battery longevity. Tesla's VPP, for example, typically maintains a minimum state of charge (usually 20–30%) and caps the number of dispatch events per year. The financial benefit generally outweighs the marginal additional wear for most systems.
What's the difference between net metering and a VPP?+
Net metering credits your bill for excess solar pushed to the grid in real time — it's a passive accounting mechanism. A VPP actively dispatches your battery based on grid conditions, pays you per event, and requires your system to respond to external control signals. They can coexist — net metering handles daily solar surplus, VPP handles strategic battery discharge.
When will homes be able to trade electricity directly without a utility program?+
Peer-to-peer energy trading and direct retail market access are being piloted in a few states (notably New York's REV initiative and some Texas microgrid projects), but mainstream availability for residential customers is likely 5–10 years out. Current regulatory frameworks require aggregators as intermediaries.
