Why Credit Card Processing Fees Are So High

Credit card fees feel high because multiple parties take a cut of each transaction. Most small business owners only see the final percentage, not the layers underneath it.

How Credit Card Fees Actually Work

A typical card payment may include interchange, card network fees, and processor markup. That means your business is paying several systems at once just to move one transaction.

Interchange vs Processor Markup

  • Interchange: paid to the card-issuing bank
  • Network fees: paid to Visa, Mastercard, or other networks
  • Processor markup: paid to the payment company handling the transaction

Why Small Businesses Pay More

  • Less negotiating power
  • Higher relative risk assumptions
  • Flat-rate pricing from providers like Stripe and Square
  • Less volume to qualify for better pricing

How Stripe and Square Price Their Services

Stripe and Square often simplify pricing into easy flat-rate models. That convenience helps businesses get started faster, but the simplicity can mean paying more than a custom or volume-based setup.

Example Breakdown of a $100 Transaction

  • Interchange: roughly $1.50 to $2.10
  • Network fees: a smaller additional amount
  • Processor markup: the remainder of the total fee

Why the System Evolved This Way

Card systems were built around risk management, bank incentives, fraud controls, and convenience. The result is a mature but expensive network that works well for many businesses, but often at a significant cost.

Strategies to Reduce Payment Fees

  • Review your pricing model
  • Compare providers
  • Evaluate ACH for some transactions
  • Consider lower-cost settlement options where appropriate

Glossary

  • Interchange: the fee paid to the issuing bank.
  • Merchant Account: the account that allows a business to accept card payments.
  • Payment Gateway: software that transmits payment information.
  • Chargeback: a disputed card transaction reversed through the card system.
