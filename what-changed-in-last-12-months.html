What changed in the last 12 months — San Diego: service, costs, and tech

TL;DR: Over the past year, local service markets and household/business economics saw modest-to-significant shifts: contractor costs and wait times rose in many trades, energy programs and rebates expanded, small businesses adopted AI tools for efficiency, and homeowners have more incentive-driven options for energy upgrades. Below is a concise guide to the top changes, what they mean, and what to do next.

SideGuy: Clarity before selling — this page documents what changed, why it's confusing, and neutral options; it is informational, not a sales pitch.

Top changes

  1. Service availability & lead times: Many trades reported longer wait times because of steady demand and constrained labor availability in the region. Expect scheduling delays for larger jobs.
  2. Pricing pressure: Material costs and labor rates increased for several common home services (HVAC, roofing, plumbing). Get multiple quotes and written estimates.
  3. Energy incentives grew: Local and state rebate programs expanded for heat pumps, efficient HVAC, and LED/lighting upgrades—some projects now offset a larger portion of cost.
  4. AI and digital tools adoption: Small businesses (including local contractors) increasingly use AI for admin, quoting, and estimates—expect more digital booking and clearer estimates, but verify details before paying.
  5. Payments and pricing models: More businesses offer online payments, digital estimates, and subscription-based maintenance plans—read the fine print on recurring charges.

What this means for you

- If you need work done, start early and expect longer scheduling windows for some trades. - Use photos and short videos to get better remote estimates (fast and more accurate). - For energy upgrades, check available rebates before you buy—contractors often can help with paperwork.

Practical steps (quick checklist)

  • Document the problem (photos + short description) to make remote quotes accurate.
  • Ask for itemized quotes and call out warranty, permit, and disposal fees.
  • Ask contractors whether they handle local rebate paperwork for energy work.
  • For urgent flooding or gas, treat as an emergency (call appropriate responders first).
  • Shop for transparent pricing and prefer contractors with local references and insurance.

Timeline / notable events

  • Mid-last-year: Several rebate programs were expanded; check SDG&E and state energy portals for up-to-date offers.
  • Fall: Hiring slowdowns in some trades made scheduling slower during peak seasons.
  • Ongoing: Small business software and AI tools matured; many service providers now use photos, text, and online scheduling for initial triage.
