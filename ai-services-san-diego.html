AI services for small teams — practical pilots, governance, and handoffs

We help small teams discover useful, low-risk AI opportunities, validate them quickly, and hand over clear operational processes so you get benefits without surprise costs or privacy headaches.

SideGuy: Clarity before selling — this page explains what to expect, how we measure outcomes, and how to minimize risk.

1) How to choose the right first use case

Pick a task that is (a) repetitive, (b) measurable, and (c) low-risk. Good examples: triaging customer messages, routing invoices, or automating simple scheduling. These have quick feedback loops so you can measure gains and iterate fast.

  • Fast feedback: choose tasks with measurable outcomes (time saved, percent automated)
  • Low friction: no heavy integrations or regulated data for the first pilot
  • High leverage: pick tasks that free up human time for higher-value work

2) Pilot approach (1–6 weeks)

We run a lean pilot: discovery (1 week), build (2–3 weeks), and a short measurement period. The goal is an MVP that proves the value, not a perfect product.

  1. Discovery: scope, success metrics, data access
  2. Build: small model / automation + integration with existing tools
  3. Measure: track defined KPIs and decide to iterate or scale

3) Data, privacy & governance

We document how data flows through the pilot, minimize sensitive data exposure, and add retention and access controls. For regulated data we add additional legal and technical checks before any live rollout.

Typical protections:

  • Minimal data use and anonymization where possible
  • Role-based access and simple logging
  • Data retention and deletion policies

4) Integration, handoff, and runbooks

We prioritize integrating with tools you already use (messaging platforms, spreadsheets, CRMs). Deliverables include a runnable automation, runbook, and training so your team can operate and maintain it.

Handoff package typically contains:

  • Technical notes and access details
  • Runbook: how to operate, monitor, and roll back
  • Training checklist for the team

5) Measuring success and scaling

Decide success criteria before the pilot. Common metrics: time saved, percent of tasks automated, response accuracy, or direct revenue impact. If the pilot meets success thresholds, we plan a phased rollout to reduce risk and operational friction.

Short case example

Example: Auto-response pilot for customer messages. Pilot reduced triage time by 72% and allowed staff to focus on exceptions. The pilot cost was $3,500; break-even happened within 6 weeks because of time savings.

Quick checklist — start a pilot

  • Identify a measurable use case
  • Pick 1–3 success metrics
  • Confirm data access and privacy constraints
  • Run a 2–4 week pilot
  • Decide: iterate, scale, or stop

FAQ

How long does a pilot take?
Typically 2–6 weeks depending on scope and data readiness.
How much does a pilot cost?
Typically $2,500–$8,000 depending on scope, integrations, and data prep.
What about data/privacy?
We design minimal-data pilots and document flows; sensitive data can be excluded or anonymized for the pilot.
Will this replace staff?
We focus on augmentation — making teams more effective and freeing people for higher-value work.
How do we measure ROI?
Define success metrics up front (time saved, accuracy, revenue) and measure them during the pilot.
